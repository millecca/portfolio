Cherish | Fall Monitoring System

Helping nursing homes staff detect and respond to falls

I worked with an early-stage startup to design a fall monitoring platform, using design research to ensure product-market fit in assisted living care facilities.

My Role
UX, UI, User Research
Timeline
4 months (Spring 2025)
Industry
Healthcare, IOT

Context

A fall detector that prioritizes privacy.

While most offerings used cameras or wearables, Cherish was building a fall monitor that used AI and radar instead. This new alternative allowed older adults to live safely without feeling like there was something getting in the way.

Nursing homes were key partners, acting as investors, consumers, and a real-world environment to test the emerging technology.

The wall-mounted device feels more like a lamp than a health monitor.

Problem

Nurses work in a high stress environment where new tools add friction.

While the tech could significantly improve patient outcomes, it only mattered if it worked with existing workflows. Delivering value meant helping nurses identify and respond to incidents as quickly as possible.

At the same time, the product was still evolving. The device captured powerful information but offered little feedback, and we had to translate this “black box” into actionable insights, accounting for the device’s limitations and functionalities that could at times be unclear.

Before After

The existing dashboard was just a table of vitals. It didn't prioritize the fall alerts—the product's entire value prop.

Research

Nurses don’t just want to detect falls. They want to prevent them before they happen.

We visited three different nursing homes to understand their workflows, challenges, and possible barriers to adoption. Numerous interviews, usability tests, and observational sessions helped us understand how nursing homes currently dealt with falling.

Residents want to move around independently, but even basic movements can lead to falls

Getting out of bed or into a wheelchair are high-risk moments in nursing homes. Nurses want to monitor these “self-transfers” without getting overwhelmed by alerts.

Staffing inconsistencies and poor communication make it harder for nurses to understand each person’s fall risk

Nurses often swap between patients, wings, and even facilities, and in the chaotic environment don’t always have up-to-date context on each resident’s fall risk.

Fall monitoring poses both a legal liability and protection for nursing homes

Nurses are worried the device’s data could be used against them in court, but knowing an injury was caused by a fall or proving a rapid response time would protect them.

Solution 1

Warning nurses about risky resident movement

The original dashboard only alerted nurses about falls that had already happened. We introduced a lower-priority alert type focusing on potential falls.

Alerts for risky movements like getting out of bed at night.

To avoid excessive alerts, nurses can modify which movements trigger alerts in a certain patient.

Solution 2

Adding patient notes and tags

Nurses can add notes for clear communication on what care a resident needs. Tags give an "at a glance" look at a patient's fall risk, whether they have a history of falling or they're a new patient whose behavior is still unclear.

Adding a note from the patient profile.

Solution 3

Keeping track of incident response times

The device can detect who's who when it comes to residents, but it could only see there was a nurse in the room, not the specific nurse. To make sure nurses were actually going to check on residents after a fall, we introduced a timeline that would show exactly when a nurse entered the room after an incident.

A green line shows when a nurse entered the room and how long they were there for.
